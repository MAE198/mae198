Lesson 1: Intro to Mechanical Engineering & Scratch Coding

The first session is designed to spark young students’ initial interest in the fields of technology and engineering. The lesson starts by touching upon broad aspects of mechanical engineering, ranging from design process to potential careers. Although virtual classrooms limited the ability to provide a physical experience, the lesson strived to incorporate a hands-on approach by having the students learn and experiment with coding. The prepared lesson varied in difficulty depending on the grade level and experience of the students. For younger groups, specifically the middle school classes, an interactive PB&J demonstration was prepared to provide a visual representation of how computers think and how it pertains to coding. This required that the demonstrator who is acting as a computer be very literal when receiving instructions, or its "code", on how to make a PB&J sandwich. The high school group received the same information, including some coding operations—variables, loops, and conditional statements—without the demonstration.

Lesson 2: Vex Robotics

The purpose of this lesson was to introduce students to the basics of how robots perform the tasks that they do, and to show first-hand, the mechanisms behind the curtain that allow a robot to perform. This lesson was also a way to implement the block coding that they learned from the first lesson into a 3D atmosphere. This lesson was a way to show how programming can take on many forms, and take on many other aspects than only computer based. Much of this lesson focused on how computers think by giving directions to a simulated robot to move around on a virtual playground. Vex Robotics also has competitions and real life parts to make real robots in real life. The coding portion of the robot was the most tangible way for students to get a sense of how robots can move through coding blocks similar to Scratch coding blocks.

Lesson 3: Isometric Drawings & 3D Modeling

Our goal for lesson 3 was to introduce and interest students in 3D printing and how computers can help engineers design 3D objects. In order to accomplish a hands-on experience, students were also introduced to Computer Aided Design (CAD). The CAD lesson starts with a brief introduction of 3D printers and CAD terminology, such as sketching, extrusion, dimensions, and geometry. For the CAD introduction, students were directed to OnShape, a free online CAD software website. Through these online softwares, students were shown how to create a sketch, extrude it, and then cut features into their 3D model.

Lesson 4: Simulations of Circuits and Arduino Coding

The objective of lesson 4 was to introduce Arduino and circuits to the students since electronics is widely used in all fields of engineering. Ostensibly, most undergraduate projects utilize Arduinos for various applications. Introducing students to a widely used engineering tool will help them be able to jump right into many extracurricular activities when they enter college. This lesson was only taught to the high schoolers and specifically to an audience of physics students. We noticed that the topics of Arduino and electricity were not introduced in middle school, so many of the topics covered would be too heavy and would be better suited for a high school audience. However, the electricity portion of the lesson could have been taught to the middle school classes, but only 3 lessons were taught to them and this lesson 4 was not.
